# Academic Register Calibration

Complement to `editorial_cleanup.md`: this defines what academic tone IS, not just what AI phrasing is NOT.

## Hedging calibration scale

| Strength | Signal verbs | Use when |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Strong | demonstrates, establishes, confirms, shows | Evidence is robust, replicated, or methodologically sound |
| Moderate | suggests, indicates, implies, points to | Evidence is suggestive but not conclusive |
| Weak | may, might, could, appears to, seems to | Evidence is preliminary, correlational, or contested |

Over-hedging (all weak verbs) reads evasive. Under-hedging (all strong verbs) reads overconfident. Calibrate to match evidence strength.

## Discipline-specific tone

- **Humanities**: allow interpretive language ("can be read as", "invites consideration of"); footnote-heavy.
- **Social sciences**: prefer moderate hedging; ground claims in method.
- **STEM**: favor strong claims with narrow scope; let data speak.

Match register to the target discipline. When uncertain, default to moderate hedging with explicit methodological caveats.

## First-person conventions

- **Acceptable**: "I argue", "we propose" when the essay is a single-author argument or collaborative position.
- **Avoid**: "I think", "I feel", "I believe" — replace with evidence-grounded phrasing.
- **Never**: "In my opinion", "Personally" — these signal subjective preference, not reasoned judgment.

When the discipline or venue discourages first person, recast: "This essay argues" or "The evidence suggests".

## Positive phrasing examples

| Avoid | Use instead |
|-------|------------|
| A lot of research shows | A substantial body of evidence indicates |
| This is really important | This finding carries significant implications |
| The data proves | The data supports the claim that |
| It is obvious that | The evidence consistently demonstrates that |
| This paper talks about | This paper examines / investigates / argues |

## Calibration pass

After editorial cleanup, verify:

1. Hedging matches evidence strength (not uniformly weak or strong).
2. Tone is consistent within each section.
3. First-person usage follows discipline convention.
4. No colloquial intensifiers remain ("really", "very", "huge", "massive").
5. Claim verbs are precise ("challenges", "extends", "refines") not vague ("deals with", "looks at").
